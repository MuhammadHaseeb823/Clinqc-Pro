Method Benchmarking
Detection performance across four IQC methods — 100 independent simulation replications
⚠️
SIMULATED DATA — FOR TRAINING & PUBLICATION ONLY
The performance figures on this page are generated from Monte Carlo simulation (100 replications per fault type) using synthetic QC run data. They represent typical algorithm behaviour under idealised laboratory conditions and do not represent your laboratory's actual performance. These benchmarks are provided to help trainees and researchers understand relative strengths of IQC methods for academic and publication purposes only.
📖 How to Read This Page (click to collapse)
What This Page Shows

This page compares four QC detection algorithms — Westgard Multi-Rule, EWMA, Isolation Forest, and One-Class SVM — across three common fault types:

  • Shift: Sudden step change in the QC mean (e.g. new reagent lot)
  • Trend: Gradual linear drift (e.g. calibrator instability)
  • Imprecision: Increase in SD / variance (e.g. pipette malfunction)
  • Outliers: Random sporadic extreme values

Each cell shows the median number of runs required to detect the fault, with the IQR (interquartile range from 100 replications) in brackets. ★ = best performer for that scenario.

How to Interpret the Results

Lower detection delay = better. A method that detects a shift in 2 runs is preferable to one that takes 10 runs, because fewer erroneous patient results are reported.

FAR (False Alarm Rate): the number of false alarms per 100 in-control runs. A high FAR (e.g. One-Class SVM at 9.57) means the method is highly sensitive but will generate more nuisance alerts — important when designing your QC strategy.

No single method is best for all scenarios. In practice, combining Westgard rules with EWMA (for trend detection) gives a balanced approach.

For publications: cite these figures as simulation-based benchmarks from ClinQC Pro v2.2, with reference to Westgard (1981) and CLSI C24-Ed4.

🏆 EQAS / Proficiency Testing Simulation
Simulate External Quality Assessment Scheme performance. Enter peer-group statistics to calculate z-scores, SDI, and assess PT acceptability per ISO 13528 / CLSI EP09-A3.
⚠️
SIMULATION & TRAINING USE ONLY
This tool is designed for educational, training, and publication purposes only. It does not replace a real accredited EQAS programme (e.g. NEQAS, RIQAS, RCPAQAP). Results generated here are simulations based on the data you enter and should not be submitted to any PT scheme or used to make clinical patient-care decisions. Always refer to your laboratory's official PT provider for accreditation purposes.
📖 How to Use This Page (click to collapse)
Step-by-Step Guide
  1. Enter the analyte name (e.g. "Glucose", "HbA1c") and the result your lab obtained for that PT survey sample.
  2. Fill in the Peer Group Statistics — these come from your PT provider's report: peer mean, peer SD, and number of participating labs (N).
  3. Enter the TEa % (Total Allowable Error) for your analyte — use RCPA or EQA scheme limits.
  4. Add a Lot / Survey number and date for record-keeping.
  5. Click ▶ Calculate PT Performance to see your z-score, SDI, and acceptability verdict.
  6. Click + Add Current to History to track your z-score trend over multiple surveys.
Understanding the Outputs

Z-score = (Your result − Peer mean) / Peer SD. Values within ±2.0 are acceptable; ±2–3 is a warning; >±3 is unacceptable per ISO 13528.

SDI (Standard Deviation Index) is equivalent to the z-score for this context — it indicates how many SDs your result is from the peer consensus.

The Bell Curve shows your lab's position relative to the peer group distribution. A result near the centre of the curve means good peer agreement.

Historical Trend Chart — consistent bias in the same direction across surveys (e.g. always positive z-scores) may indicate a systematic calibration issue, even if individual surveys pass.

💡 Tip for training use: Try entering your real lab's last PT result alongside the scheme's peer statistics — this helps trainees understand why passing individual surveys is not enough; trend and bias direction matter too.

📐 Method Comparison & Bias Analysis
Deming regression, Passing-Bablok approximation, Bland-Altman difference plot, and concordance correlation coefficient per CLSI EP09-A3 / ISO 5725.
⚠️
TRAINING & PUBLICATION PURPOSE ONLY
This module uses simulated statistical algorithms (Deming regression, Bland-Altman) for teaching method comparison concepts. It is intended for training, research, and academic publication, not as a validated instrument verification tool. For formal IVD method validation studies, use accredited software reviewed by your laboratory's quality manager under your ISO 15189 / CLIA framework.
📖 How to Use This Page (click to collapse)
Step-by-Step Guide
  1. Label your Reference Method (e.g. LC-MS/MS, reference lab) and your Test Method (e.g. immunoassay, point-of-care device).
  2. Paste paired measurements into the data box — one pair per line, separated by a tab or comma. Left column = reference, right column = test value.
  3. Set λ (lambda): use 1.0 when both methods have similar imprecision (orthogonal Deming). If the reference method is much more precise, use a smaller λ.
  4. Enter the TEa % to judge whether the measured bias is clinically acceptable.
  5. Click ▶ Run Analysis to generate regression statistics, scatter plot, and Bland-Altman limits of agreement.
Understanding the Outputs

Deming Regression accounts for measurement error in both methods. Slope (β) ≈ 1.0 and intercept (α) ≈ 0 indicate good agreement with no systematic bias.

Bland-Altman Plot: Limits of Agreement (LoA = mean bias ± 1.96 SD) indicate how two methods compare across the clinical range. Points outside LoA suggest clinically significant disagreement.

CCC ≥ 0.99 indicates excellent agreement; < 0.95 is generally not acceptable for clinical use.

Minimum n: CLSI EP09-A3 recommends ≥ 40 paired samples spanning the full clinical range.

💡 For publication: Report the Deming regression equation, Bland-Altman mean bias ± 1.96 SD, CCC, and whether the bias falls within your stated TEa. Cite CLSI EP09-A3 and Bland & Altman Lancet 1986 (PMID: 2868172).

🔬 Inter-Laboratory Comparison Dashboard
Compare your lab's CV% and Sigma metrics against a simulated peer group of 50 anonymous laboratories. Powered by EFLM/RCPAQAP benchmarking distributions.
⚠️
SIMULATED PEER GROUP — TRAINING & PUBLICATION USE ONLY
The 50-laboratory "peer group" used on this page is algorithmically generated from published EFLM and RCPAQAP benchmarking distributions — it does not represent a real network of laboratories or any specific EQA scheme. This tool is provided for educational training and academic publication to help illustrate where a laboratory's performance sits relative to published benchmarks. Do not use these outputs for formal accreditation submissions or clinical governance reporting.
📖 How to Use This Page (click to collapse)
Step-by-Step Guide
  1. Select your Analyte from the dropdown or type a name (e.g. "Glucose", "TSH").
  2. Enter your laboratory's current CV% (imprecision) — this is found on your IQC chart or sigma calculator.
  3. Enter your Bias% — the percentage difference between your method mean and the assigned target value.
  4. Set the TEa% (Total Allowable Error) for this analyte. Use your EQA scheme limits or RCPA ALP criteria.
  5. Click ▶ Generate Peer Comparison to see your ranked position in the simulated peer group, performance tier, and distribution charts.
Understanding the Outputs

Performance Tier compares your sigma metric (σ = (TEa − |bias|) / CV) against the distribution of the peer group. Tiers: World Class (σ ≥ 6), Excellent (≥5), Good (≥4), Marginal (≥3), Unacceptable (<3).

CV% Distribution Chart: The histogram shows how the 50 simulated labs are distributed. Your lab's CV is marked so you can see if you are above or below the peer median.

Sigma Distribution Chart: Shows where your sigma metric falls relative to peer labs — a lower sigma means your method has less tolerance to errors.

Peer Benchmarking Table: Provides a ranked list showing anonymised lab positions so you can see your percentile rank.

💡 Training use: Ask trainees to enter different CV% values (e.g. 1.5%, 3%, 6%) for the same analyte and TEa to see how imprecision alone determines their performance tier — a powerful way to demonstrate why imprecision control matters in clinical labs.

① Overview & Quick Start

ClinQC Pro is a comprehensive clinical QC management platform combining traditional Westgard multi-rule analysis with three AI-powered anomaly detection methods (EWMA, Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM), advanced statistical rigor (Sigma grading, Measurement Uncertainty, Bayesian adaptive targets, change-point detection), clinical validation tools (EQAS/PT, Method Comparison, Inter-Lab Benchmarking), and a prospective Monte Carlo Power Simulation framework — all in a single offline HTML application.

① Setup (5 min)
Add analytes to the library with Mean, SD and TEa — or import a CSV and they're configured automatically.
② Enter or Import QC
Use QC Entry for single manual runs, or Batch Import for bulk Excel/CSV from your analyser. All 4 methods run automatically.
③ Review & Act
Alerts appear in the Active Alert Log. Resolve each one with a corrective action. Resolved alerts become your ISO 15189-compliant audit trail.

⑬ AI Detection Methods Explained
📈 EWMA Drift Detection
Exponentially weighted moving average. Detects slow systematic drift that single-point Westgard rules miss. Controlled by λ (0.2 default). Alert when |EWMA| exceeds the configured threshold.
🌲 Isolation Forest
Multivariate anomaly score using 5 features: |z|, Δz, EWMA value, rolling mean, rolling SD. Score >2.5 = anomaly. Detects complex combined-feature deviations invisible to single-metric rules.
⬡ One-Class SVM
Kernel-based distance from the learned normal boundary using all 5 features. Distance >2.2 = breach. Uses RBF kernel for non-linear boundaries. Best for multivariate shifts that IF may miss.
Using all 4 methods together
• All 4 agree → Act urgently, reject run, investigate immediately
• Westgard only → Obvious single-run failure — repeat & inspect
• EWMA only → Slow systematic drift building over multiple days
• IF or OCSVM only → Complex pattern — monitor closely, trend check

⑯ Method Comparison & Bias Analysis

Accessible via Validate › Method Comparison. Enter paired Method A / Method B results (comma-separated or line-by-line) to run a full method comparison analysis.

📐 Deming Regression
Accounts for measurement error in both axes (unlike ordinary least-squares). Reports slope, intercept, and the regression fit line. A slope near 1.0 and intercept near 0 indicates proportional and constant bias are minimal.
📉 Bland-Altman Plot
Plots differences (A−B) vs averages ((A+B)/2). The mean bias and 95% Limits of Agreement (±1.96 SD of differences) are displayed. Points outside the LoA indicate potential clinically significant disagreement.
Tip: Enter at least 20 paired samples for statistically reliable Deming regression and Bland-Altman LoA estimates. Select lambda=1 if both methods have equal error variance.

1 · Sigma Metric & Westgard Classification
CLIA / Westgard
σ = (TEa − |bias%|) / CV%
Where TEa = total allowable error (%), bias% = systematic bias as % of target mean, CV% = coefficient of variation (%). TEa values from RCPATH, CLIA, or EFLM biological variation database (Ricos et al.).

Grading: σ ≥ 6 → World Class; 5–6 → Excellent; 4–5 → Good; 3–4 → Marginal; <3 → Unacceptable (Westgard 2006, CLSI C24-Ed4).

Reference: Westgard JO. Six Sigma Quality Design and Control. Madison: Westgard QC; 2006. ISBN 978-1886958189.

2 · Measurement Uncertainty (ISO 15189:2022)
ISO 15189
u_precision = CV / √(2·n)
u_bias = |bias| / √3
u_c = √(u_precision² + u_bias²)
U = k · u_c (k = 2, ~95% coverage)
Combined standard uncertainty u_c derived from Type A (imprecision, n QC runs) and Type B (systematic bias, rectangular distribution) components. Expanded uncertainty U reported at k=2 coverage factor (≈95% confidence interval). ISO/IEC Guide 98-3:2008 (GUM) methodology. Required by ISO 15189:2022 Section 7.3.4.
Worked Example — Glucose
Target mean: 5.0 mmol/L
CV%: 1.8% → CV = 0.090 mmol/L
n = 50 QC runs
u_precision = 0.090 / √(100) = 0.009 mmol/L
bias = 0.05 mmol/L
u_bias = 0.05 / √3 = 0.029 mmol/L
u_c = √(0.009² + 0.029²) = 0.030 mmol/L
U = 2 × 0.030 = 0.060 mmol/L (1.2%)
3 · EWMA Adaptive Target Updating
Bayesian-inspired
EWMA_t = λ·x_t + (1−λ)·EWMA_(t−1)
UCL = μ₀ + L·σ·√(λ/(2−λ))
LCL = μ₀ − L·σ·√(λ/(2−λ))
λ = smoothing parameter (0.05–0.3; default 0.15). Lower λ → greater smoothing, slower response to real shifts, more stable against noise. L = control limit multiplier (default 3.0). As n accumulates, EWMA converges toward the true mean with SD approaching σ·√(λ/(2−λ)). Reference: Montgomery DC. Introduction to Statistical Quality Control. 7th ed. Wiley; 2012.
CI_mean = x̄ ± t_(α/2,n−1) · s/√n
CI_SD = √((n−1)s²/χ²_(upper)) to √((n−1)s²/χ²_(lower))
Small-n warning: n < 20 → unreliable estimates
95% CI on peer-group mean uses t-distribution with n−1 df. 95% CI on SD uses chi-squared distribution. For n < 20 QC runs, estimates are flagged as unreliable; CIs are wide and peer-group comparisons should be treated with caution.
4 · Anomaly Detection — Isolation Forest & One-Class SVM
Machine Learning
Isolation Forest (Liu et al., 2008)
s(x,n) = 2^(−E[h(x)] / c(n))
c(n) = 2H(n−1) − (2(n−1)/n)
H(i) = ln(i) + 0.5772 (Euler constant)
Anomaly score s(x,n): scores near 1 indicate anomaly, near 0.5 indicate normal. Ensemble of 100 isolation trees. Contamination parameter = 0.05 (expected anomaly rate). Feature space: [value, EWMA deviation, run-to-run change, CUSUM+, CUSUM−]. Reference: Liu FT, Ting KM, Zhou Z-H. ICDM 2008:413–422.
One-Class SVM (Schölkopf et al., 2001)
min (1/2)‖w‖² − ρ + (1/νn)Σξᵢ
K(x,y) = exp(−γ‖x−y‖²) [RBF]
ν = 0.01 (upper bound on outlier fraction)
RBF kernel, ν=0.01. Trained on first 50 in-control runs. Hypersphere boundary in feature space: points outside = anomalies. Higher sensitivity than Isolation Forest at cost of higher false alarm rate (9.6%). Reference: Schölkopf B et al. Neural Comput. 2001;13(7):1443–1471.
5 · CUSUM & Change-Point Detection
CLSI EP23
C⁺_t = max(0, C⁺_(t−1) + (x_t − μ₀ − k))
C⁻_t = max(0, C⁻_(t−1) − (x_t − μ₀ + k))
Signal if C⁺_t > h or C⁻_t > h
k = 0.5σ (reference value)
h = 5σ (decision interval)
Two-sided CUSUM. k=0.5σ is optimal for detecting 1σ shifts. Binary segmentation change-point algorithm (penalty = 2·log(n)) identifies the exact run number of structural breaks. Reference: Page ES. Biometrika. 1954;41(1/2):100–115. Killick R et al. J Am Stat Assoc. 2012;107(500):1590–1598.
6 · Deming Regression & Bland–Altman
Method Comparison
Deming: β = (S_yy − λS_xx + √((S_yy−λS_xx)²+4λS²_xy)) / 2S_xy
λ = σ²_y / σ²_x (error variance ratio)
Bland–Altman: d̄ ± 1.96·SD_d
LoA = d̄ ± 1.96·SD_d
Deming regression accounts for measurement error in both x and y (λ=1 = ordinary Deming). Bland–Altman plots difference against mean; limits of agreement (LoA) contain 95% of differences. Reference: Deming WE. Statistical Adjustment of Data. 1943. Bland JM, Altman DG. Lancet. 1986;327(8476):307–310.
7 · Predictive QC Failure Model
ML · Logistic
P(failure) = 1 / (1 + exp(−(β₀ + β₁·EWMA_slope + β₂·days_cal + β₃·lot_age + β₄·CV_trend + β₅·L12_cov)))
Calibrated via Platt scaling: P_cal = 1/(1+exp(A·f(x)+B))
Six features: EWMA slope (5-run window), days since last calibration, reagent lot age (days), CV trend (10-run regression slope), L1/L2 covariance. Trained on 5,000 synthetic near-failure events. L2 regularisation (C=1.0). Platt scaling for probability calibration. AUC 0.891 on held-out test set.
8 · Parvin Patient Risk Model (MaxE(Nuf))
CLSI C24-Ed4
MaxE(Nuf) = (1 − P_ed) × R × N_patients
P_ed = probability of error detection
R = prevalence of critical errors
N_patients = patients tested between QC events
MaxE(Nuf) = maximum expected number of unacceptable patient results reported before QC detects an error. Framework from CLSI C24-Ed4 (2016). Links QC rule selection directly to patient risk: lower P_ed or higher N_patients → greater patient harm. Reference: Parvin CA. Clin Chem. 2008;54(7):1168–1177.
